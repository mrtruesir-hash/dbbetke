Betting tax in Kenya explained

Two taxes affect Kenyan bettors: excise duty on the money you stake, and withholding tax on your winnings. Here's how each one works, in plain language.

Please note: This is general information for players, not tax or legal advice. Betting tax rates in Kenya are set by law (the Finance Act) and have changed more than once recently — the Finance Act 2025 reduced both rates to 5%, while a Finance Bill 2026 proposes raising withholding tax on winnings back to 20%. Because the rate in force can change between when this page is published and when you read it, we deliberately don't quote a single fixed number here — always confirm the current rate with the Kenya Revenue Authority (KRA) (kra.go.ke) or a qualified adviser before relying on any figure.

1. Excise duty on your stake

Kenya charges an excise duty on the amount you stake (the money you place on a bet). It is usually deducted automatically — so a small part of each deposit or stake goes to tax before your bet is placed. You don't file this yourself; it's handled by the operator.

Example (illustrative only — check KRA for the real current rate): if the excise rate were 5%, staking KES 100 would send roughly KES 5 to excise duty, leaving about KES 95 on your bet. The actual rate depends on which Finance Act provision is in force when you play.

2. Withholding tax on your winnings

Kenya also charges a withholding tax on gambling winnings. This is deducted from your winnings (not your stake) before the payout reaches you, so what you withdraw is already net of this tax.

Example (illustrative only — check KRA for the real current rate): if the withholding rate were 20%, winning KES 1,000 in profit would mean about KES 200 withheld and around KES 800 reaching your account. Again, confirm the actual current rate with KRA — it has moved between 5% and 20% within the last year.

What this means for you

  • You don't usually file anything. Both taxes are typically deducted at source by the operator, so you don't have to submit them yourself.
  • "Winnings" means profit. Withholding tax applies to the amount you win, not the stake you already paid.
  • Your withdrawal is already net of tax. The amount you cash out has had withholding tax applied.
  • Rates change. Kenya's betting taxes have been adjusted several times — check the current figures before relying on them.

Bet smart, whatever the tax

Tax is just one part of the maths. The bigger factors in the long run are staying disciplined, understanding odds and markets, and only betting what you can afford. See our responsible gaming guide.

Where to check official rates

For the current, official position, refer to the Kenya Revenue Authority (KRA) and the latest Finance Act. If you have a complex situation, speak to a qualified tax adviser.
